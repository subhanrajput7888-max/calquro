Understanding Percentages

Percentages are everywhere in our daily lives - from calculating tips and discounts to understanding interest rates and statistical data. Mastering percentage calculations is essential for academic success and practical life skills.

Basic Percentage Formula

Percentage = (Part/Whole) √ó 100

This fundamental formula is the foundation for all percentage calculations.

Common Percentage Calculations

1. Finding a Percentage of a Number

Formula: (Percentage/100) √ó Number

Example: What is 25% of 80?
Solution: (25/100) √ó 80 = 20

2. Finding What Percentage One Number is of Another

Formula: (Part/Whole) √ó 100

Example: What percentage is 15 of 60?
Solution: (15/60) √ó 100 = 25%

3. Finding the Whole When Percentage and Part are Known

Formula: Part √∑ (Percentage/100)

Example: If 20% of a number is 15, what is the number?
Solution: 15 √∑ (20/100) = 75

Percentage Increase and Decrease

Percentage Increase

Formula: ((New Value - Original Value) / Original Value) √ó 100

Example: Price increased from $50 to $65
Increase = ((65 - 50) / 50) √ó 100 = 30%

Percentage Decrease

Formula: ((Original Value - New Value) / Original Value) √ó 100

Example: Price decreased from $80 to $60
Decrease = ((80 - 60) / 80) √ó 100 = 25%

Advanced Percentage Techniques

Compound Percentages

When percentages are applied multiple times, use compound calculations:

Formula: Final Value = Initial Value √ó (1 + r)^n

Where r = percentage rate (as decimal), n = number of periods

Percentage Points vs. Percentage Change

Percentage Points: Direct difference between percentages
Percentage Change: Relative change in percentages

Example: Interest rate changes from 5% to 7%
- Percentage point increase: 2 percentage points
- Percentage increase: (7-5)/5 √ó 100 = 40%

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Confusing percentage points with percentage change
  2. Forgetting to convert percentages to decimals
  3. Mixing up the order in percentage calculations
  4. Not considering the base value correctly
  5. Rounding too early in complex calculations
